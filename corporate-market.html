Corporate Market

The Bridge of Order: Institutional Capitalism, growth, and global supply chains.

I. Corporate Governance (Facts 01-10)

01

Fiduciary Duty: Executives are legally obligated to maximize shareholder profits.

02

Hierarchical Management: Strict top-down control from CEO to entry-level staff.

03

Board of Directors: External investors oversee company direction and executive pay.

04

Proprietary Knowledge: Heavy reliance on patents, trademarks, and trade secrets.

05

Mergers & Acquisitions: Consolidation of firms to achieve market power.

06

Stock Options: Using equity to align employee performance with shareholders.

07

Performance Metrics: Using KPIs to drive worker productivity.

08

Outsourcing: Moving operations to lower-cost regions to maintain margins.

09

Lobbying: Large firms influence government policy to favor business growth.

10

Brand Equity: Massive investment in marketing to create intangible asset value.

II. Economic Globalization (Facts 11-20)

11

Multilateral Trade: Support for the WTO and regional trade blocs (e.g., USMCA).

12

Offshore Finance: Use of international tax havens to manage corporate capital.

13

Supply Chain Optimization: Just-in-time manufacturing to reduce inventory costs.

14

Foreign Direct Investment: Corporations investing in developing nations' infrastructure.

15

Currency Arbitrage: Profiting from fluctuating national currency values.

16

Global Labor Market: Treating labor as a commodity to be sourced efficiently.

17

Standardization: Pushing for global technical and legal standards (ISO).

18

Special Economic Zones: Areas with reduced taxes to attract big business.

19

Transnational Law: Private arbitration for disputes between states and firms.

20

Export-Led Growth: Prioritizing international markets over domestic consumption.

III. Financialization (Facts 21-30)

21

The Big Three: Massive influence of asset managers like BlackRock and Vanguard.

22

Quarterly Capitalism: Prioritizing short-term reports over long-term stability.

23

Derivatives Trading: Complex instruments used to hedge risk or speculate.

24

Central Bank Symbiosis: Interest rate policies often mirror corporate needs.

25

Too Big to Fail: Concept that certain firms are vital to national stability.

26

Credit Ratings: Private agencies (Moody's) determining the "health" of nations.

27

Stock Buybacks: Using cash to purchase own shares, inflating stock value.

28

Venture Capital: Private funding for high-growth tech startups.

29

Debt Financing: Relying on corporate bonds and loans to expand.

30

Algorithmic Trading: Using high-frequency AI to execute millions of trades.

IV. State & Market (Facts 31-40)

31

Public-Private Partnerships: Private firms managing public toll roads or bridges.

32

Regulatory Capture: When an industry gains influence over its oversight agency.

33

Subsidies: Government tax breaks for "strategic" industries (oil, tech).

34

Privatization: Turning state utilities (water, power) over to private firms.

35

Contracting Out: Governments hiring private firms for civil services.

36

Anti-Trust (Selective): Using law to manage competition without killing power.

37

Intellectual Property: Aggressive enforcement of copyright and patents.

38

Economic Sanctions: Using corporate trade as a weapon of state policy.

39

Corporate Welfare: Direct state aid to prevent large-scale bankruptcies.

40

Austerity: Cutting social spending to maintain state credit ratings.

V. Theory & Ideology (Facts 41-50)

41

Neoliberalism: Dominant school of thought favoring markets and property.

42

Washington Consensus: Policy prescriptions for "developing" countries.

43

Supply-Side Economics: Belief that cutting business taxes drives prosperity.

44

The Invisible Hand: Modern interpretation of self-interest benefiting society.

45

Human Capital Theory: Viewing health/education as productivity investments.

46

Public Choice Theory: Applying economic logic to government behavior.

47

Managerial Revolution: Shift of power from owners to professional managers.

48

Chicago School: Influential academics (Friedman) who pushed deregulation.

49

Trickle-Down Theory: Idea that wealth at the top eventually benefits everyone.

50

Global Hegemony: Goal of creating a unified, market-based global order.
